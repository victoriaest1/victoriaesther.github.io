The Challenge

Making finance feel human

Most finance apps feel clinical, intimidating, or outright boring. The brief was to design something people would actually want to open every day — an app that makes tracking money feel like a habit worth keeping, not a chore to avoid.

The core tension: the app needed to be data-dense enough to be genuinely useful, yet light and approachable enough that non-finance people wouldn't bounce after day three.

"I tried three budgeting apps and deleted them all. They made me feel judged."
— User interview participant, age 26

The Solution

Warm cards, clear hierarchy, delightful motion

A card-based interface with a warm palette keeps data readable without feeling like a spreadsheet. Every interaction has a micro-animation — spend categories expand with a spring, monthly summaries slide in on scroll, and the onboarding flow uses a single-question-per-screen pattern to reduce cognitive load at the critical first-use moment.

The design system was built mobile-first with 80+ components — every spacing value, colour, and text style tokenised so a developer could implement it without guesswork. Dark mode was included from day one in the token structure, not retrofitted later.

Reflections

What I'd do differently

Motion design takes longer than expected. I underestimated the time needed to spec micro-interactions properly. Next time I'd budget double the time for motion and do a dedicated animation sprint.
Users revealed a feature I hadn't designed. Testing showed people wanted a quick-add gesture (swipe from edge) that wasn't in the original spec. Catching it in testing early enough meant I could include it before the prototype was locked.
Tokens saved hours mid-project. When the brand colour shifted slightly, updating a single token propagated the change across all 80+ components instantly. Never build without tokens again.
