The Challenge

A dashboard where everything competed for attention

The existing product had grown organically over two years. Every team had added their own metrics until the dashboard held 40+ data points with no visual hierarchy — everything the same size, the same weight, the same priority. Users were spending an average of 8 minutes per session just locating the three KPIs they actually cared about daily.

"I open this dashboard every morning and it stresses me out. I can never find what I need without clicking around forever."

The brief was clear: redesign the dashboard experience so users can surface their most critical data within 90 seconds of opening the app, while still preserving access to the full data depth for power users.

I started with 6 user interviews and analysis of existing session recordings to understand what users were actually trying to accomplish. The key insight from card sorting: users naturally grouped metrics by urgency, not by the product categories the engineering team had used to build the sidebar.

This reframing — from product-centric to user-task-centric — became the foundation of every design decision that followed.

The Solution

Progressive disclosure, at speed

The redesigned dashboard introduces a three-layer information architecture: a persistent top strip for the user's 3 pinned critical KPIs, a main content area with colour-coded metric clusters, and a collapsible deep-dive panel for the data power users need. Everything else lives in a clean secondary view.

Each colour cluster maps to a specific job-to-be-done — not to a product category. Users can pin their most important metrics to the persistent top bar in one click, and a new "Daily Digest" view shows only anomalies and alerts, reducing noise by 80%.

Usability testing ran across 5 sessions with real users from the target persona group. All 5 completed the primary task within 90 seconds in the redesigned prototype, compared to an average of 8 minutes in the original.

Key Learnings

What I'd do differently

Involve engineers earlier. A filter component I designed in week two had a performance implication nobody caught until handoff. Earlier pairing would have saved a redesign round.
Test at lower fidelity. I spent too long polishing the hi-fi before testing. The core layout insights would have surfaced just as clearly from wireframes, freeing more time to iterate on the details that actually mattered.
Don't underestimate onboarding. Users loved the new layout but needed a brief tutorial to discover the pinning feature — a tooltip system would have reduced the learning curve significantly.
