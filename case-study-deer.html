Overview

A gallery that slows you down

CERFS (French for "deer") is a single-page editorial web design concept built entirely in Figma. The brief was self-imposed: design a nature photography showcase that feels as calm and intentional as the subject matter — deer in their natural habitat across the forests of Switzerland.

Rather than a traditional grid gallery, the design takes cues from editorial print magazines and Swiss International Style — strict grids, purposeful white space, expressive typography, and a colour palette rooted in the natural world. The result is a page you scroll slowly, not scan quickly.

The Challenge

How do you design for stillness?

Most photography galleries are designed for efficiency — quick browsing, large grids, filters. But wildlife photography, especially of deer, carries a different quality. The subject demands patience. The viewer should feel invited to slow down, not rush through.

"

In stillness, the deer reveals what haste conceals.

— Design principle guiding every layout decision

The design challenge was to translate that quality of stillness into a visual language — through layout rhythm, typographic pacing, and the way photos are revealed as you scroll.

  • Create a single long-scroll experience that rewards slow browsing
  • Use Swiss grid principles to create structure without sterility
  • Introduce warmth through a gold accent alongside the naturalistic greens
  • Design a horizontal scroll section so photos feel discovered, not presented
  • Make typography as expressive as the photography itself

Design Process

From concept to one long frame

The entire design was built inside a single Figma frame — a deliberate choice. Rather than designing individual sections in isolation, working in one connected canvas forced every section to feel like part of a continuous scroll, not a collection of separate screens.

01
Mood & Reference
Gathered references from editorial magazines (Kinfolk, The Plant, Cereal) and Swiss design archives (Müller-Brockmann, Ruder). Defined three mood pillars: romantic, editorial, organic.
02
Colour Palette
Natural mid-green (#4A7A4A) as primary, warm gold (#C9965A) as accent, cream (#F5EDD8) as background. Three distinct palettes: light cream sections, dark green sections, and light sage sections — creating visual rhythm as you scroll.
03
Typography Pairing
Playfair Display (display headings, italics, poetic asides) paired with Inter (labels, captions, body). The contrast between serif romance and sans-serif precision mirrors the tension between wild nature and designed space.
04
Layout Architecture
12 sections in a single 1440 × 6612px frame: nav → hero → editorial intro → 3 gallery grids → horizontal scroll strip → quote → full-bleed → about → closing → footer. Each section alternates between light and dark backgrounds to create breathing room.
05
Horizontal Scroll Strip
A fixed-height viewport (700px) containing an inner frame 6600px wide — 6 full-width photos side by side. In Figma prototype mode, this scrolls horizontally. Each photo has an italic Playfair Display caption and gold sub-label, making discovery feel curated.
06
Whimsical Details
Decorative rings and circles floating behind sections, gold corner brackets on photo frames, ✦ stars as ornamental dividers, Latin name Cervus elaphus, Swiss coordinates 46°49'N · 7°09'E, Roman numeral section labels, and poetic asides scattered throughout.

Key Design Decisions

Every choice, intentional

✦ Decision 01
One frame, not many screens
Instead of designing section by section, the entire page lives in a single 1440 × 6612px Figma frame. This forces continuity — colours have to transition, sections have to breathe into each other, and the page reads as one composition rather than a collection of separate components.
✦ Decision 02
Horizontal scroll for the gallery strip
A horizontal scroll section within a vertical-scroll page creates a moment of surprise — the user's gesture changes. The photos are 1100px wide so you only ever see a glimpse of the next one, encouraging exploration without showing everything at once.
✦ Decision 03
Gold as warmth, not luxury
Gold (#C9965A) was chosen specifically because it references afternoon light and warm soil — not prestige or status. It pairs with the naturalistic greens to feel earthy rather than opulent, and appears only as accents: rules, dots, corner marks, and sub-labels.
✦ Decision 04
Roman numerals as section markers
Using I, II, III... instead of 01, 02, 03 adds an archival, almost literary quality — as if each section is a chapter in a book rather than a screen in a UI. It reinforces the editorial nature of the project without being visually heavy.

Reflections

What this project taught me

🏗️
Design in context
Working in a single long frame forces you to see every section in relation to the ones before and after. You can't make Section 6 beautiful in isolation — it has to work in the flow.
✍️
Typography as composition
Using type at radically different scales — 260px display vs 10px labels — creates the same kind of visual rhythm that photographs do. Type isn't just readable, it's compositional.
🌿
Constraints breed character
Limiting the palette to two families and three colours forced every decision to be purposeful. The constraints of Swiss design aren't restrictions — they're a creative framework.
✦
Details make the difference
The Latin name in the nav, the coordinates in the hero, the Roman numerals — no user will notice these consciously. But they add up to a feeling of care and intentionality that elevates the whole.
